## Plaint Filed Under Section 26 and Order VII Rule 1 of the Code of Civil Procedure

### 1. Foundational facts and beneficiary status

The Alummoottil family, an Ezhava family from Muttom, Haripad, constituted the 1st Defendant trust, Alumoottil Tharavad Trust, vide *Trust Deed No. 2/IV/2007*, registered on 04/01/2007, at SRO Cheppad. The Deed has designated the Trust Beneficiaries as the lineal **descendants of Alumoottil Chellamma Channathy**. The Plaintiff is the grandson of Chellamma through her fourth son Radhakrishnan and is therefore a **rightful Beneficiary of the Trust**, clearly substantiated by his birth certificate, and corroborated by the 2nd Defendant's admission in *O.S. 243/2024*. No testamentary validation is required to establish the Plaintiff's Beneficiary status. The Defendants have, on multiple occasions, expressly admitted and affirmed the Plaintiff's status as a Beneficiary—both ceremonially, by inviting him to perform the lamp lighting at the 23/12/2024 inauguration, and in official documents, such as *O.S. 243/2024*, where it is stated that the Plaintiff is *"the son of Mr. Radhakrishnan M, the 2nd Trustee."* These repeated acknowledgments undermine the credibility of all subsequent denials of his rights.

### 2. Historical occupation and right of access

The Plaintiff resided in the northwestern bedroom on the ground floor of the Meda until age twelve (12). The Plaintiff attended schools in Nangiarkulangara and Mavelikkara and has **regularly and peacefully visited his ancestral home for decades**, exercising his rights under *Section 57 of the Indian Trusts Act, 1882*, and the right to property under *Article 300A of the Constitution of India*. There is no provision in the Trust Deed that expressly authorizes any Trustee or individual to block a Beneficiary's lawful access. On December 23, 2023, at the Meda's inauguration, Defendant No. 4 publicly invited the Plaintiff to light the ceremonial lamp before over 150 people, openly acknowledging him as a Beneficiary—directly contradicting later claims of trespass. The actions of Defendant Nos. 2 to 4—including concealing Trust Deed 2/IV/2007, forging disputed documents, orchestrating the filing of false FIRs, and deploying unauthorized enforcers, culminating in O.S. 243/2024 filed on 14/08/2024—collectively demonstrate a concerted effort to dispossess the Plaintiff. The Plaintiff seeks a declaration of Beneficiary status with enforceable rights to the Meda, Nalukettu, and Nelpura, which will prevent further obstruction and ensure enforcement of the Trust Deed. This exclusion results from a deliberate chain of events. After the Plaintiff requested transparency at a Trust meeting on December 23, 2023, he was met with hostility and threats. Following the filing of O.S. 84/2024 on March 6, 2024, the Defendants accelerated property transfers and increased the use of private enforcement. On August 9, 2024, they physically attacked and barred the Plaintiff from his home. FIR 574/2024 and O.S. 243/2024 then followed in succession as retaliation. The Defendants exploited the Plaintiff's U.S. residence to secure *ex parte* orders (fabrication of cause of action in O.S. 243/2024) and adverse rulings by denying him notice, a fair hearing, and by manipulating court procedures to their advantage.

### 3. Suppression of Trust Deed

The 2nd Defendant has attempted to erase the Plaintiff's Beneficiary status by suppressing *Trust Deed 2/IV/2007* (in *O.S. 243/2024*), the original of which is in the possession of the 2nd Defendant. This deed is essential to establishing the Plaintiff's rights. The Plaintiff's written requests for copies dated 03/10/2024 and 28/10/2024 were ignored, and his third request on 05/12/2024 was expressly refused. This violates *Section 57 of the Indian Trusts Act, 1882*, which mandates Trustees must provide Beneficiary access to the Trust instruments. In further demonstration of documentary diligence and good faith, the Plaintiff made prompt payment to the SRO Cheppad for production of true copies of 2/IV/2007, 66/IV/2022, and 17/IV/2024.

### 4. O.S. 84/2024 and seizure of Trust property

On 06/03/2024, the Plaintiff filed *O.S. 84/2024* before this Hon'ble Court seeking injunctive protection of his right to access his ancestral Trust property buildings and his childhood home — Meda, Nalukettu, and Nelpura. Despite the ongoing proceedings of case *O.S. 84/2024* in Court, the 3rd Defendant has unlawfully seized control of Trust property. He issued orders to unauthorized third-party enforcers—outsiders without legal or fiduciary status—stating: *"that no visitor is allowed to enter the premises without (his) consent or accompanied by one of the other four Trustees."* His decree to the Plaintiff reads: *"No visitor can enter the premises without my permission or being with one of the other four Trustees,"* and *"For the foreseeable future, as long as I am around, no [...] activities will go on at Alumoottil unless I approve. [...] I will not allow you unfettered access there unless you are accompanied by your father. [...] I can hold your father accountable, not you."* This action constitutes a blatant violation of Trust law and the established Trust Deed. By resorting to physical enforcement through unauthorized third-party enforcers, this conduct perilously converts the Trust into a self-serving clandestine syndicate. Such actions are fundamentally at odds with a Family Trust's lawful and ethical management, undermining its integrity and purpose. This flagrant defiance and utter disregard for the law demand an unequivocal rebuke from the Court.

### 5. The 09/08/2024 attack — premeditated retaliation

The very acts warned of in *O.S. 84/2024*—namely, obstruction of access, physical exclusion, and abuse of private force—were executed by the Defendants, demonstrating that the subsequent attack and the filing of *O.S. 243/2024* were not isolated events but the premeditated realization of those prior warnings.

Despite the matter being *sub judice*, the 2nd and 3rd Defendants retaliated with a private campaign of enforcement, initiated immediately after the Plaintiff's demand for transparency at the 23/12/2023 trust meeting. On 09/08/2024, Defendant Nos. 2 to 4 deployed five unauthorized third-party enforcers—Arun B. Unni (Defendant No. 5), V. Nandakumar (Defendant No. 6), B. Nadarajan (Defendant No. 7), Stalin, and Bobi (other unauthorized third-party enforcers)—to physically bar the Plaintiff from the Meda. Unauthorized third-party enforcers, who do not hold any legal role in the Trust as Trustee, Author, or Beneficiary, were on the secure gated Trust property to assault the Plaintiff. These enforcers attacked the Plaintiff in front of the Meda, a Trust property, the Plaintiff's childhood home, and the subject of *O.S. 84/2024*, currently *sub judice*.

Defendant Nos. 2 and 3 claim that entry was restricted due to ongoing renovations. It is submitted that the Meda was inaugurated on 23/12/2023, following completion of all renovation activities. Over one hundred fifty (150) attendees gathered on its first floor (above ground) that day, confirming its safety and accessibility. Both *Mathrubhumi* and *Malayala Manorama* report that following completion of renovation works, the Meda was scheduled to open to the public at large on or around 07/07/2024. Video footage from the inauguration shows that all major renovations had been completed. News articles also confirm this fact. These records directly contradict the later claim that any significant ongoing renovation justified restricting Beneficiaries' access on 09/08/2024.

### 6. Unauthorized delegation of fiduciary duties

This attack was premeditated and orchestrated by Defendant Nos. 2 to 4, in retaliation for the filing of *O.S. 84/2024*. They hired these unauthorized third-party enforcers to stage a violent incident, falsely portraying the Plaintiff as aggressive to justify their scheme to exclude him from the Trust property. The Plaintiff, who came from the USA to visit his childhood home, was blocked by these unauthorized third-party enforcers from entering the Meda. The enforcers' actions on Trust premises were orchestrated and facilitated by Defendant Nos. 2 to 4, and this constitutes unlawful delegation of fiduciary duties in breach of *Section 47 of the Indian Trusts Act, 1882*, which states that *"a Trustee cannot delegate his office or any of his duties... unless the Beneficiary consents."* The 3rd Defendant's instruction to the 5th Defendant—stating *"no visitor is allowed without my consent or accompanied by one of the other four Trustees"*—is illegal for three reasons:

**a.** Defendant No. 3's "Trustee" status is only documented on the forged *Amendment No. 17/IV/2024* and not in any other public document accessible to Beneficiaries.

**b.** Defendant No. 3 (holder of OCI Card Number A1172154, permanently residing in the USA) is disqualified from serving as Trustee under *Section 60 Explanation 1*, which excludes "a person domiciled abroad" from being a Trustee; and

**c.** *Section 57* grants the Beneficiaries of a Trust the absolute right to access trust records and instruments. Neither the law nor the Trust Deed authorize any individual (Trustee, Author, or another Beneficiary) to block the Plaintiff's lawful access as a Beneficiary into his ancestral and childhood home.

### 7. Pre-notification and interference with judicial process

At no time prior to the 09/08/2024 incident was the Plaintiff ever notified, warned, or served with any restraining court order barring his entry to the Trust property. The Plaintiff notified Defendant No. 2 well in advance through his father Co-Trustee Radhakrishnan M.

The 3rd Defendant's letter issued during the pendency of *O.S. 84/2024*, attempted to interfere with the judicial process by restricting the Plaintiff's access to Trust property, matter that was pending adjudication. Such actions undermine the integrity of these proceedings and warrant appropriate judicial censure. In ***Biswanath and Anr. v. Shri Thakur Radhaballabhji & Ors. [1967] 2 S.C.R. 618***, the Honourable Supreme Court held that Trustees cannot act against the trust's object or deny Beneficiaries their rightful interest. In ***Goswami Shri Mahalaxmi Vahuji v. Ranchhoddas Kalidas and Ors. [1970] 2 S.C.R. 275***, it was held that Trustees may not treat trust property as personal dominion or arbitrarily exclude Beneficiaries. ***Sheikh Abdul Kayyum v. Mulla Alibhai [1963] 3 S.C.R. 623***, confirmed that fiduciary principles under the *Indian Trusts Act* apply to all trusts.

### 8. The Plaintiff's account of 09/08/2024

On 09/08/2024, at 10:30 PM, the Plaintiff had lawfully approached Alumoottil Meda, having notified the 2nd Defendant through Co-Trustee Radhakrishnan. Defendant Nos. 5 to 7 were stationed outside, the door was locked, and Defendant No. 5 refused to provide the key. Defendant Nos. 5–7, as well as Bobi and Stalin taunted the Plaintiff to enter the Meda. The Plaintiff, in response to being barred entry, symbolically protested by kicking the locked door, causing neither damage nor unauthorized entry. This act, as confirmed by video evidence, was nonviolent and resulted in no property loss or breach (which, at most, could be considered a minor property dispute). Instead, Defendants Nos. 5–7, acting under instructions from Defendants Nos. 2–4, initiated violence and physically restrained the Plaintiff, causing grievous hurt (torn rotator cuff requiring surgery), punishable under *Section 117(2), Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita, 2023*. This premeditated physical restraint was planned to provoke *FIR 574/2024*. Defendants, in bad faith, omit in *O.S. 243/2024* that it was the Plaintiff who called the police for protection. Video and eyewitness statements confirm no damage or entry. Defendants Nos. 2–4 filed no FIR or police complaint for trespass or mischief, exposing that the sole purpose of the incident was to fabricate *FIR 574/2024*. Call records will show coordination between Defendant Nos. 2–7, constituting organized crime. Sworn statements and the FIR that Plaintiff lodged (*FIR 956/2024*) against third-party enforcers confirm the unprovoked attack. Video shows no construction at the Meda, debunking Defendants' claims. Meanwhile, Defendant No. 3 demanded ₹5 lakh for fictitious damage, amounting to extortion. These acts violate *Section 14, Indian Trusts Act, 1882*, and *Articles 14 and 21 of the Constitution of India*. ***Zoroastrian Coop. Housing Society Ltd. v. District Registrar [2005] 3 S.C.R. 592*** bars Trustees from using force or delegating exclusionary power. The Plaintiff's visit to the Meda on 09/08/2024, was pre-notified to the Defendants, who had ample opportunity to raise any objections in advance but chose instead to ambush and physically restrain him.

### 9. Forged Amendment Deeds

Without notifying or obtaining consent from any Beneficiary, the 2nd Defendant forged *Amendment Deeds 66/IV/2022 and 17/IV/2024* and registered them at SRO Cheppad. These documents unilaterally altered the Trust's core tenet, the definition of the Trust's Beneficiaries. The 2nd Defendant's exclusive presence—via his photograph, signature on all pages, and role as the sole "PRESENTANT" in both forged amendments—demonstrates a deliberate attempt to manipulate the trust's structure. The original *Trust Deed 2/IV/2007* does not contain any clause authorizing amendments by Trustees or Authors without unanimous Beneficiary consent. *Section 11 of the Indian Trusts Act, 1882* requires Trustees to *"obey the directions of the author of the trust given at the time of its creation, except as modified by the consent of all the Beneficiaries being competent to contract."* Defendant No. 2 deceitfully asserted before this Honourable Court in *O.S. 243/2024* that his forged amendment is the Trust's real *"constituting"* document, under the misguided belief that this Honourable Court would remain unaware of the Plaintiff's rightful Beneficiary status. In altering the Trust Beneficiaries without consent or legal basis, Defendant No. 2 also violated *Section 49*, which provides that *"where a discretionary power... is not exercised reasonably and in good faith, such power may be controlled by a principal Civil Court of original jurisdiction."* The unlawful creation, concealment, and use of these forged amendments to alter the definition of Beneficiaries constitutes forgery within the meaning of civil law principles. Defendant No. 2's forged Amendments, lacking Beneficiary consent, are void, as the Supreme Court nullified such amendments in ***Official Trustee, West Bengal v. Sachindra Nath Chatterjee [1969] 3 S.C.R. 92***.

### 10. O.S. 243/2024 — fraudulent suit

*O.S. 243/2024*, filed by the 2nd Defendant, is a fraudulent suit aimed at excluding the Plaintiff by concealing *Trust Deed 2/IV/2007*. *O.S. 243/2024* Paragraph 4, pages 2–3 falsely claims the Plaintiff *"doesn't have any connection with the trust,"* *"is not a member,"* and *"has no right to interfere,"* despite admitting in the same suit that the Plaintiff is *"the son of Mr. Radhakrishnan M, the 2nd Trustee"* confirming his lineage as a lineal descendant of Chellamma Channathy and his lawful Beneficiary status under *Trust Deed 2/IV/2007*. This contradiction exposes the suit's *mala fide* intent and violates *Section 17*, which prohibits Trustees from favoring one Beneficiary over another. In ***S.P. Chengalvaraya Naidu v. Jagannath [1993] Supp. (3) S.C.R. 422***, the Supreme Court held that litigation based on suppression of foundational documents constitutes fraud upon the Court and must be dismissed as a nullity under *Order VII Rule 11 of the CPC*. Each day that *O.S. 243/2024* remains pending in this Honourable Court serves as a perpetuation of fraud and manipulation of the judicial process.

### 11. FIR 574/2024 — three-step scheme

*FIR 574/2024* was a three-step scheme to (i) reverse blame for the attack on 09/08/2024, (ii) exclude and exonerate the 6th and 7th Defendants from responsibility for the attack, and (iii) falsely label the Plaintiff a trespasser, for their civil case. ### 12. Contradictions between FIR 574/2024 and O.S. 243/2024

No weapon was recovered, and medical reports show no injuries, despite the alleged attack to the *"chest with a rock."* The core contradiction between FIR 574/2024 and O.S. 243/2024 is this: the FIR states that the Plaintiff did not enter the Meda, while O.S. 243/2024 alleges trespass. **Trespass is alleged despite no entry—a physical and legal impossibility.** Furthermore, logically, a Beneficiary and family member cannot trespass into his own ancestral and childhood home. Interestingly, neither the Trust nor the Managing Trustee found it necessary to file a police complaint or FIR for this *"trespass"*. Contradictions in FIR 574/2024 do not end there — Defendant Nos. 6 and 7, the assault leaders, are completely omitted in *FIR 574/2024*. The Plaintiff's own complaint was ignored, and *FIR 956/2024* was filed only after Plaintiff's escalation to the Chief Minister's Citizen's Grievance Portal. Defendant Nos. 5–7, Bobi, and Stalin illegally video recorded the Plaintiff, and Defendant No. 3 distributed this video to the public. Though the footage exonerates the Plaintiff, Plaintiff reserves the right to file suits for defamation and violation of privacy (both civil and criminal). Notably, despite the accusations of trespass and mischief, the Defendants did not file any police complaint or FIR for trespass against the Plaintiff at any time. This absence of a bona fide complaint is consistent with the retaliatory and pretextual nature of their narrative in *O.S. 243/2024* and *FIR 574/2024*.

The 6th Defendant repeated the 3rd Defendant's claim to the police officers verbatim, showing premeditation and coaching. This is civil fraud and abuse of process under *Order VI Rule 16 and Order VII Rule 11 of the CPC*.

### 14. Missing wildlife heirlooms

On 23/12/2023, during the Meda's inauguration, as Defendants No. 2 and 3 addressed 150 guests, the Plaintiff discovered the alarming absence of two wildlife heirlooms—a mounted wild bison skull and a pair of deer antlers—which had long been affixed to the first floor since his childhood. These artifacts, enshrined in *Schedules I and III of the Wildlife (Protection) Act, 1972*, bear irreplaceable cultural and historical weight. Since 2007, Defendant No. 2 has maintained overall custody of all Trust buildings and heirlooms. However, from 2020 to 2023, Defendant No. 3 exercised control specifically over the Meda under a Power of Attorney granted for the duration of its renovation. Yet, neither Defendant 2 nor 3 reported the disappearance to the Forest Department or police, nor pursued recovery—brazenly flouting *Sections 15 and 68 of the Indian Trusts Act, 1882*, and *Sections 39 and 62 of the Wildlife Act*. When the Plaintiff demanded answers, Defendant No. 3 and Bimal Chandran Madhavan—Defendant No. 2's son—responded with taunts, while Defendant No. 4 revealed that Defendant No. 3 and Bimal had spirited the artifacts to a foreign location, mocking that even *mashiyittu nokkal* (a superstitious practice involving ink done to trace missing items) would prove futile. This admission establishes their constructive possession and raises a concern that the artifacts may have been removed from India without proper authorization. If such export occurred, it could constitute a cognizable offense under *Section 51 of the Wildlife Act*. The existence of the wildlife artifacts is substantiated by photographs and witness testimony. The Plaintiff further submits, to the best of his knowledge and belief, that these items may also be reflected in court inventories, such as *O.S. 197/1983* from the Subordinate Court, Mavelikkara, subject to verification. The Supreme Court's ruling in ***Shanti Vijay & Co. v. Princess Fatima Fouzia [1980] 1 S.C.R. 459*** is unequivocal: unexplained loss of trust property constitutes a breach *per se*. The Plaintiff, therefore, respectfully seeks this court to direct the Defendants to immediately restore these heirlooms to the Meda.

### 15. 18-year failure to maintain accounts

Since the Trust's registration on 04/01/2007, the 2nd Defendant, as Managing Trustee, has persistently failed to disclose audited accounts, ledgers, or financial statements, breaching *Sections 19, 57, and 59 of the Indian Trusts Act, 1882*, which mandate continuous transparency and Beneficiary access to Trust records. Given the Trust's control over high-value ancestral assets, receipt of foreign inward remittances, and substantial renovation expenditures, the Trust—registered as No. 2/IV/2007—is presumed to have, or ought to have, obtained tax-exempt status under *Section 12A of the Income Tax Act, 1961*, requiring annual filings via *Form 10B* from 2007 to 2024. If foreign funds were accepted without valid *12A registration or FCRA approval*, such transactions may constitute violations under the *Foreign Exchange Management Act, 1999*, particularly *Section 3*, read with relevant *RBI notifications*. These are not technical oversights, but statutory breaches with potential criminal implications.

Separately, the Plaintiff contributed ₹2.4 lakhs between 2005 and 2011 to Defendant No. 3's account for the maintenance of the Meda and Nalukettu, establishing legal standing to demand full Trust accounts for the 2nd Defendant's entire tenure. Despite these contributions, Defendant Nos. 2 and 3 diverted Trust funds to finance litigation—including *O.S. 243/2024*—aimed at excluding the Plaintiff, a lawful Beneficiary, thereby violating *Section 17* (which bars favoring one Beneficiary over another) and *Section 14* (which prohibits Trustees from acting adversely to Beneficiaries' interests). The Plaintiff's formal requests for accounts dated 03/10/2024 and 28/10/2024 were ignored, and a registered letter dated 05/12/2024 was returned marked *"refused,"* confirming deliberate obstruction of statutory access. In ***L. Janakirama Iyer v. P.M. Nilakanta Iyer [1962] Supp. (1) S.C.R. 206*** and ***Sri Vedagiri Lakshmi Narasimha Swami Temple v. Indurl Pattabhirami Reddy [1967] 1 S.C.R. 280***, the Hon'ble Supreme Court held that a Trustee's accounting obligation is continuous and non-delegable. In ***Shanti Vijay & Co. v. Princess Fatima Fouzia [1980] 1 S.C.R. 459***, the Court ruled that unexplained loss of Trust property or records constitutes breach *per se*. The 2nd Defendant's sustained concealment of accounts and misuse of Trust funds over the last eighteen (18) years—including disappearance of heirlooms, unauthorized land acquisition, and persistent accounting non-compliance—now compels full judicial scrutiny and disclosure to prevent further malfeasance.

### 16. Fraudulent cause of action in O.S. 243/2024

*O.S. 243/2024* is a fabricated suit founded on falsehoods and forged documents. No police complaint was filed despite such "reliable information"; instead, the suit relies exclusively and entirely on *FIR 574/2024* lodged by an Arun B Unni (unauthorized third-party enforcer). In ***A.V. Papayya Sastry v. Government of A.P. [2007] 3 S.C.R. 603***, the Honourable Supreme Court held that where fraud is the foundation of a suit, the entire proceeding is a nullity and must be struck off under *Order VII Rule 11 of the CPC*. *O.S. 243/2024* satisfies the test and is liable to summary dismissal. Filing such a frivolous suit with Trust funds against a Beneficiary is a violation of *Section 18 of the Indian Trusts Act 1882*, which stipulates that Trustees must prevent waste.

### 17. Private fiefdom and conflict of interest

Defendant No. 2 has turned the Trust into a private fiefdom. Along with Defendant No. 4, he acquired land directly abutting the Meda, creating a private perimeter that now functions as a chokepoint. These acquisitions were made without disclosure to Beneficiaries, or this Honourable Court. This conduct violates *Sections 51 and 66 of the Indian Trusts Act, 1882*. *Section 51* prohibits a Trustee from using trust-property *"for his own profit,"* and *Section 66* entitles the Beneficiary to *"a charge on the whole fund"* if trust-property is wrongfully mingled with private assets. In ***Krishnaji Ketkar v. Mahomed Haji Latif & Ors. [1968] 3 S.C.R. 862***, the Supreme Court held that fiduciaries acquiring adjacent land for personal control of trust property is a significant conflict of interest, and a grave breach of Trust.
