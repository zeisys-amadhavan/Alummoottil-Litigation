## The three objectives

### Objective 1 — Criminal accountability against D2

Drive a **Section 215 BNSS reference** by the Munsiff Court to a Magistrate of First Class, founded on D2's conduct across the connected suits: perjury (S.229 BNS) on the 16/02/2026 affidavit; using known-false evidence (S.233); fabrication (S.240) covering O.S. 243/2024's false cause of action and false registration number, and — once inspected — the Minutes Book and the "Nomination as beneficiary"; false statement in a verified plaint (S.235); false information to a public servant (S.217); and criminal breach of trust by a fiduciary (S.316(5)). This is **independent of CMP 2828/2024** (the assault prosecution).

### Objective 2 — Removal of D2 as Managing Trustee, with leverage settlement

Seek removal under **Section 73 of the Indian Trusts Act**. The criminal exposure from Objective 1 is the lever that drives D2 to settle: D2 resigns, the Plaintiff is appointed Managing Trustee, and D2 and his children renounce their beneficiary interests. **Caveat:** the Plaintiff's USA residence raises the same Section 60 Explanation 1 ITA question used against D3; counsel must structure the appointment relief — relocation, an Indian-domiciled co-trustee, or another mechanism.

### Objective 3 — Shut down Sreemoolavasam with maximum financial loss to the defendants

Void the **27/10/2025 lease** on three independent grounds:
- **Registration Act ss. 28/49** — registered at Marad (outside SRO Cheppad's territorial jurisdiction; the property lies in Cheppad). Frame as "seriously questionable / lacking jurisdiction," **never "void"** — wrong-office registration does not automatically void a lease.
- **Section 52 Transfer of Property Act** — executed during the pendency of the suit (*lis pendens*).
- **Section 11 ITA / Clause 17(c) of the 2022 Amendment** — no beneficiary consent to leasing.

Then an interim injunction stopping commercial operations. On voidness/eviction, occupants become trespassers with **no recovery of sunk costs**: Reply Para 7(b) admits Sivadasan funded the renovation from personal accounts "under no obligation" to disclose, which forecloses a restitution claim; **S.51 ITA** bars trustee profit; **S.66 ITA** gives the beneficiary a charge on mingled funds; **disgorgement under S.23 ITA**. Collateral pressure: ROC complaints (ss.184/188 Companies Act) and GST invalidation (GSTIN 32ABQCS9416G1Z1).

## Cross-cutting drafting discipline

These are the rules that keep the position sharp; they apply in every filing in every forum.

1. **Quote verbatim or flag as inference.** "R2 swore X" is factual; "R2 committed perjury" is the court's conclusion to draw, not ours to assert.
2. **"I have not seen X" ≠ "X does not exist."** Never argue from silence (D2's WS, the unread resolutions, the Minutes Book contents).
3. **Do not dignify void documents by arguing from their content.** The 2022/2024 Amendments, the Nomination, and the Marad registration are challenged on the concept — used only as admissions against interest (e.g. Clause 17(c) of the 2022 Amendment admits beneficiary consent is required for leasing).
4. **Do not gather confirming evidence where the defendants have already admitted (S.58 IEA)** — it risks surfacing disconfirming facts. Don't pull the SRO Cheppad encumbrance certificate, don't obtain a certified copy from Marad, don't pull MCA filings unless a Section 88 self-dealing argument needs them.
5. **Marad / Section 28 stays at "seriously questionable / lacking jurisdiction," never "void."**
6. **No numerical scoring** of the litigation position.
7. **Beneficiary-status argument belongs in the plaint/submissions, not the contradictions matrix.**
8. **Self-dealing is NOT pleaded** — Sreemoolavasam's directors appear (per public records) to be strangers; let the lease stand as a governance/accounting question regardless of ownership.
9. **Radhakrishnan** is confirmed alive, hostile, and a future defendant — not a witness or co-plaintiff here.
